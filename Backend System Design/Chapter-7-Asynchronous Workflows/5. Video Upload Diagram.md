1. In a video processing system, why is it important to separate the source video storage from the converted video storage?
	1. Separating storage helps keep things organized and secure. The source video storage doesn't need to be publicly accessible and can be deleted once conversion is complete since source videos are very large. The converted video storage is where the final videos are served from and should be kept separate to avoid mixing temporary source files with the final deliverables.
2. **Why should video conversion tasks be delegated to a worker pool instead of having a single service handle all conversions?**
	1. Converting videos is a very expensive task, especially when dealing with multiple formats (4K, HD, etc.). Using a worker pool allows for parallel processing, prevents a single service from becoming a performance bottleneck, and enables better scalability. Each conversion becomes a separate job that can be processed independently.
3. **What information should be passed to worker processes in a video conversion system, rather than passing the actual video data?**
	1. Workers should receive a message containing: the job to be done, where to find the video (e.g., blob storage location), and who to notify when the job is complete. This prevents services from becoming stateful by holding large video files in memory, which could cause failures.
4. **In a distributed video processing architecture, why is it problematic to have the notification service track the completion status of multiple conversion jobs?**
	1. Having the notification service track job completion adds unwanted state to that service. If the notification service goes down, all tracking information about job completion is lost. Workers don't track this information either, so there would be no way to know which jobs completed. It's better to have job completion notifications write back to a database through the appropriate services.
